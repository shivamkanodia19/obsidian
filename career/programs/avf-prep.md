# Aggie Venture Fund — Second Round Interview Prep

## What is AVF?

Student-led venture capital fund at Texas A&M. Mission: invest in breakthrough technologies to radically improve millions of lives. They are an actual fund — not a paper portfolio club — which means they make real investment decisions and take VC seriously.

Key distinction from Aggie Investment Club (AIC): AIC = public equity. AVF = early-stage startups / venture capital.

---

## What They're Looking For (Second Round)

By Round 2, fit is assumed. They know you can talk. Now they're testing:

### 1. Investment Judgment
- Can you evaluate a startup and form a view quickly?
- Do you understand what makes a company fundable vs. a good idea?
- Can you defend your thesis under pushback?

### 2. VC Literacy
- Do you understand how a fund actually works (LP/GP, carry, fund lifecycle)?
- Do you know the stages: pre-seed, seed, Series A/B?
- Can you speak to valuation without traditional DCF (since early-stage rarely has cash flows)?

### 3. Sourcing / Pattern Recognition
- How would you find good deals?
- What markets excite you and why?
- What's a trend you'd bet on in the next 5 years?

### 4. Pitch Deck Analysis
- Round 2 at student funds often includes: review a deck, tell us if you'd invest, why/why not
- Be ready with: market size, team, traction, competitive moat, business model critique

### 5. Why VC / Why AVF
- Generic "I want to help startups" fails. Connect it to your actual experience.
- Your angle: Co-Founder of ClinicalHours (you've been pitched AND are building), FEDVT research (data-driven decision-making), engineering background (technical diligence).

## VC Fundamentals Primer

### Fund Structure

```
LPs (capital providers)
  → pension funds, university endowments, family offices, corporations
  → provide the money, hands-off on decisions

GPs (fund managers)
  → make investment decisions
  → earn 2% management fee (annual) + 20% carried interest (profit share)

Portfolio Companies
  → receive the capital in exchange for equity
```

**The math:** If a $10M fund returns $50M → GPs keep 20% of $40M profit = $8M carry

### Investment Stages

| Stage     | Company State             | Typical Check Size |
| --------- | ------------------------- | ------------------ |
| Pre-seed  | Idea / MVP                | $100k–$500k        |
| Seed      | Early traction            | $500k–$3M          |
| Series A  | Proven model, scaling     | $3M–$15M           |
| Series B+ | Growth / market expansion | $15M+              |

Student funds typically focus on **pre-seed to seed** — earliest and hardest to evaluate.

### How a VC Evaluates a Startup

**The 4 core lenses:**
1. **Market** — Is it big enough? (TAM/SAM/SOM) Is it growing?
2. **Team** — Do they have unfair advantage? Domain expertise + execution history?
3. **Product** — Is there real differentiation? What's the moat?
4. **Traction** — Revenue, users, growth rate, retention — any signal of product-market fit?

**Early-stage valuation** (no DCF) — typically done by:
- Comparable deals (what did similar-stage co's raise at?)
- Rule of thumb: pre-money = post-money - check size; post-money = check size / ownership %
- Vibes + negotiation at very early stages (pre-seed)

### Key Terms to Know Cold

| Term                       | Definition                                                     |
| -------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Cap table**              | Ownership breakdown of a company                               |
| **Dilution**               | Your % ownership decreasing as new shares are issued           |
| **Pro-rata rights**        | Right to invest in future rounds to maintain % ownership       |
| **Lead investor**          | Sets terms, writes biggest check, takes board seat             |
| **Term sheet**             | Non-binding document outlining investment terms                |
| **Liquidation preference** | VCs get paid before founders in an exit                        |
| **Anti-dilution**          | Protects VC if future round is at lower valuation (down round) |
| **SAFE**                   | Simple Agreement for Future Equity — common at pre-seed        |
| **Convertible note**       | Debt that converts to equity at a future round                 |

### Deal Flow → How VCs Find Deals

```
Sourcing → Screening → Diligence → Term Sheet → Close → Portfolio Support
```

- **Sourcing:** inbound (pitches), outbound (cold reach), warm intros (network), accelerators
- **Screening:** ~10 min to decide if worth a call — most get cut here
- **Diligence:** market research, founder calls, customer calls, competitive analysis, financials
- **Portfolio support:** intros, hiring, next round fundraising

## Questions to Ask Them

Logistical / "how does this actually work" questions — shows you're serious about committing, not just collecting resume lines:

- What does the time commitment look like week-to-week?
- How are analysts assigned to deals — do you self-select or get assigned?
- What does onboarding look like for a new member?
- How often does the full team meet, and what happens in those meetings?
- Is there a structured curriculum or do you learn mostly by doing?
- What's the best way to add value quickly as a new analyst?
- How many active deals is the fund typically evaluating at once?
