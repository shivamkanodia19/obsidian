# ClinicalHours Visual Strategy - 2026-05-19

## Goal

Turn the ClinicalHours TikTok slideshow from a dark, sparse website recap into a brighter, tighter, more addictive mobile marketing asset.

This note is most relevant when a task explicitly wants the brighter student-facing direction represented by the current `v3bright7` export branch.

Future agents should treat that branch as one proven student system, not the mandatory visual shell for every new student slideshow.

## What the research says

### 1. The first frame has to do most of the stopping

- TikTok's ad guidance says to land the hook early, introduce the proposition in the first `3` seconds, and prioritize the first `6` seconds for recall and watch time.
- For slideshow logic, this means slide `1` cannot be a bland category statement. It must name a recognizable pain or a sharp payoff.

### 2. Structure beats decoration

- TikTok's creative guidance repeatedly uses `hook -> body -> close`.
- The ClinicalHours retention notes say each swipe should feel more useful and more specific, not more abstract.
- Therefore every slide needs one job, and the proof has to deepen as the carousel moves forward.

### 3. Mobile attention is guided by hierarchy before taste

- Nielsen Norman Group's visual-design principles emphasize scale, contrast, hierarchy, and balance.
- Mobile saliency research found a strong bias toward the top-left of the display, plus strong attention to text and images; raw color or size matter less when hierarchy is weak.
- This means the most important claim should live high on the slide, read fast, and have a direct path to the proof.

### 4. Color should attract, but readability still wins

- Research in the *International Journal of Research in Marketing* found that color complexity can increase engagement on social media images by pulling users from shallow to deeper processing.
- But NN/g also warns that contrast must hold up on mobile and in bright light.
- The practical implication is not "make it louder." It is "use richer color fields and stronger accents while keeping text contrast unmistakable."

### 5. Short-form typography can be expressive if it stays readable

- Eye-tracking research on advertising typography found that more figurative or stylized short verbal cues can increase attention and ad liking.
- The same study warns that readability remains the control variable.
- So headlines can feel bolder and more distinctive, but body copy and CTAs should stay clean and easy.

### 6. Proof images should carry information, not just mood

- NN/g advises against decorative images on mobile when they do not add informational value.
- ClinicalHours' own strategy docs say the screenshot should do more work than styling.
- So the slides should use real product surfaces as the hero evidence, with design only amplifying them.

### 7. Low-friction CTA beats premature pressure

- TikTok guidance recommends strong CTAs, but ClinicalHours' own strategy says the close should stay calm and credible.
- Cold viewers do not want to be shoved straight into `sign in`.
- A better close is an easy action that feels reversible: browse, explore, preview, then save progress later.

### 8. Reuse the lane, not the shell

- A new student issue branch should not automatically inherit the last student palette, card geometry, and screenshot framing.
- Before designing, compare the last `2-3` student exports in the same lane and identify what would feel visually repetitive.
- Keep the color story relevant and calm, but shift at least two of these when the branch is new:
  - palette family
  - screenshot framing pattern
  - card geometry
  - background structure
  - typography pairing
- If the issue is about memory, saving, or follow-through, a more organized visual metaphor can be stronger than another airy promo gradient.

## Applied design rules for the remake

1. Use a brighter editorial palette instead of the heavy dark-tech stage.
2. Put the main claim in the upper-left safe zone, where mobile attention tends to start.
3. Limit each slide to one headline and one supporting sentence.
4. Fill the frame with at least one oversized proof asset so the slide never feels empty.
5. Use controlled color complexity: warm gradients, vivid accents, and layered cards.
6. Pair a distinctive headline font with a neutral, readable body font.
7. Turn screenshots into stacked proof cards instead of leaving them stranded in blank space.
8. Keep the story in one lane: student discovery and activation.
9. Avoid empty-state screens as standalone heroes unless they are cropped into a stronger system story.
10. End on `browse as guest` / `start exploring`, not a hard login demand.
11. Do not clone the current bright cream-blue shell into every student deck; pick a distinct but brand-relevant identity for each durable branch.

## Variation rule for future branches

The brighter student-facing direction is not a universal shell for every ClinicalHours deck.

Before building a new branch:

- review at least `2-3` nearby export sets
- note which palette, font stack, proof-card treatment, and progress chrome they already use
- pick a different visual system unless the assignment explicitly wants continuity with that branch

Good variation does not mean random styling. It means choosing a fresh but relevant system:

- discovery story: cleaner search/map energy
- impact-story branch: quieter notebook/editorial energy
- clinic-ops branch: operational clarity and proof density

Keep the colors relevant, calm, and readable. Do not change the palette just to be different.

## Crop quality rules

Proof crops should feel intentional and mobile-readable.

- Crop to the control, metric, or text that actually supports the claim.
- Do not leave large dead areas around the proof just because the screenshot is truthful.
- Do not clip the key label or button that makes the screenshot understandable.
- If the crop still reads as generic empty state, cut a tighter derivative or pick a different proof surface.
