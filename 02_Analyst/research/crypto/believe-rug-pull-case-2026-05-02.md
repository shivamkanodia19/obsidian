# Believe / LaunchCoin Alleged Rug-Pull Case

Date of review: 2026-05-02

## Bottom Line

The strongest current rug-pull-style case I found is the Solana-based Believe ecosystem tied to Ben Pasternak and the tokens PASTERNAK, LAUNCHCOIN, and BELIEVE.

Important distinction: this is not a court-confirmed fraud finding yet. It is an active, evidence-heavy civil case plus current reporting that describes a classic rug-pull pattern: hype, public promises, insider enrichment, dilution, forced migration, token collapse, and project abandonment.

## Why This Case Stands Out

- Active federal class action filed on 2026-03-23 in the Southern District of New York
- Multiple token iterations tied to the same founder and platform
- Allegations of false claims about founder ownership and buybacks
- Allegations of insider token allocation, unlocked treasury, and forced migration dilution
- Platform volume and fee extraction described as very large relative to retail losses
- Token price collapse of roughly 99.8% from prior highs by late April 2026
- Ongoing public scrutiny and current reporting as recently as 2026-04-23

## Core Thesis

If you want one current crypto project to study as a likely rug-pull or scam-pattern case, study Believe.

Based on the complaint and recent reporting, the alleged scheme follows a recognizable pattern:

1. Founder launches a token and frames it as community-aligned.
2. Publicly makes trust-building claims such as minimal insider ownership and future buybacks.
3. Keeps retail holders engaged through repeated optimistic messaging while price weakens.
4. Forces migration into a new token structure that allegedly dilutes existing holders and destroys late migrators' balances.
5. Extracts fees throughout the cycle while token value collapses.
6. Goes largely silent after the damage is done.

## Timeline

- 2025-01: Pasternak launches the original token on Clout, later rebranded Believe.
- 2025-01 to 2025-10: Complaint alleges repeated public promises that platform revenue would support token buybacks.
- 2025-10-15: Complaint says a forced migration to a new token structure was announced.
- 2025-10-29: Complaint says the delayed buyback launched only after the migration and that Pasternak later went silent.
- 2026-01-13: Complaint says the official @believeapp account stopped posting.
- 2026-03-23: Federal class action filed in SDNY.
- 2026-04-23: Decrypt reports current lawsuit details and says the token had fallen about 99.8% from its prior high.

## Why It Looks Rug-Pull-Like

- Trust-building public statements appear central to the marketing
- Insider economics were allegedly obscured
- Token migration appears to have changed the deal after holders were already trapped
- Existing holders were allegedly diluted while insiders benefited
- Consumers who missed deadlines allegedly lost everything
- Fees were allegedly collected throughout the process
- Public communications dropped off after the collapse

## Counterpoints And Uncertainty

- These are still allegations in active litigation, not final findings of fact
- I did not independently verify every on-chain transaction in the complaint
- A severe token collapse alone does not prove a rug pull
- Some details in press coverage are derivative of the lawsuit rather than independently discovered

## Risk Judgment

My judgment as of 2026-05-02: avoid entirely.

Even if a court never uses the exact phrase "rug pull," the combination of alleged misrepresentation, insider-friendly dilution, destroyed balances, fee extraction, and abandonment behavior is enough to treat this as a do-not-touch case.

## What To Monitor Next

- Any motion to dismiss or substantive court rulings in Lee v. Pasternak
- Whether the Believe platform resumes normal public operations or remains effectively abandoned
- Any credible on-chain rebuttal from defendants showing the complaint's wallet clustering or fee-extraction story is wrong
- New reporting from Decrypt, major legal publications, or court-record summaries

## Practical Use

Use this case as a screening template. If a new token shows:

- anonymous or personality-driven leadership
- changing token names or migrations
- vague buyback claims
- no clear liquidity lock or treasury transparency
- heavy social hype with weak product reality
- insider wallet concentration

then treat it as potentially following the same playbook.
