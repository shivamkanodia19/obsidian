## Findings First

The previous slides were not failing because they lacked effort. They were failing because they were not yet optimized around the actual psychology of swipe-through on TikTok.

The strongest slideshow for Felt should:

- open with a sharp, answerable tension
- resolve that tension quickly enough to build trust
- reward each swipe with more specificity, not just more decoration
- stay product-led so the viewer believes the claim
- feel premium and controlled, not loud or casino-coded

The wrong way to chase retention would be:

- fake hype
- gambling-coded urgency
- oversized copy with weak proof
- tiny screenshots used as decoration
- bait without payoff

The right way is:

- curiosity with proof
- contrast with restraint
- narrative pacing
- visible usefulness
- social and mastery loops that feel real

## Accuracy Note

Most official TikTok guidance available publicly is ad-focused rather than organic-photo-mode focused. That means:

- the exact ad product specs do not automatically equal organic posting mechanics
- the attention principles still transfer well

Safe transfer from official TikTok sources:

- hook quickly
- front-load the message
- use coherent multi-image storytelling
- keep key elements away from risky edges and UI overlap
- make images legible and visually varied

Unsafe overclaim to avoid:

- pretending ad format rules are identical to organic slideshow publishing rules

## What The Current Research Actually Says

### 1. TikTok rewards fast signal clarity

Official TikTok creative guidance says:

- communicate the key message in the first 6 seconds
- front-load the action or storyline
- keep spoken or written message density manageable
- design within safe zones so key elements are not obscured

For a slideshow, the practical translation is:

- the first slide must justify the first swipe
- every slide must be readable in under 2 seconds
- no slide should need motion to make sense

### 2. Multi-image creative works best when the sequence tells one story

TikTok's image ads and carousel materials emphasize:

- telling a coherent story
- maintaining visual variety across frames
- creating anticipation from one image to the next
- keeping key elements centered enough to avoid cropping issues

For Felt, this means the 5 slides should not act like 5 separate posters.

They should behave like one sequence:

1. problem
2. simplification
3. proof of control
4. proof of improvement
5. reason to return

### 3. Curiosity works when uncertainty feels answerable

Psychology research on curiosity consistently points toward a useful pattern:

- curiosity rises when there is a gap between what the viewer knows and what they expect they can learn
- pure novelty is not enough
- uncertainty without clear reward becomes friction

Applied to Felt:

- slide 1 should create a gap
- slide 2 should reduce uncertainty fast
- the slideshow should feel progressively more informative

Bad open loop:

- "something big is coming"

Good open loop:

- "home games need a home"

The second one makes the viewer expect a concrete product answer.

### 4. Visual hierarchy still does most of the cognitive work

NN/g visual design principles emphasize that attention is guided through:

- size
- color
- contrast
- proximity
- negative space
- alignment

For Felt:

- the headline must win first glance
- the hero screenshot must win second glance
- the support crop must only exist if it adds proof
- gold should be rare enough to mean something

### 5. Contrast matters more on dark backgrounds than designers often assume

W3C contrast guidance is accessibility guidance, not marketing guidance, but it is still useful here because low-contrast dark-mode slides lose speed and legibility.

Applied to Felt:

- headline text should be very high contrast
- subtext should still clear comfortable readability
- screenshot borders, glows, and shadows should separate the UI from the background without muddying it

### 6. TikTok's recommendation logic favors strong interest signals, not generic noise

TikTok's own explanation of the For You system highlights strong interest signals such as:

- completions
- replays
- engagement signals
- signals stronger than weak metadata alone

For a slideshow, the closest transferable logic is:

- make the viewer want the next slide, not just admire the current slide
- give enough information to feel rewarding
- do not make the viewer work too hard

That is why a slideshow should build momentum through resolved curiosity, not pure mystery.

## The Psychological Tactics That Best Fit Felt

Ranked by usefulness for this project:

### 1. Curiosity Gap With Fast Payoff

Use:

- slide 1 creates a clean question
- slide 2 answers it quickly

Reason:

- curiosity gets the swipe
- payoff earns the next swipe

### 2. Tension Then Relief

Use:

- start with friction
- immediately show order

Reason:

- contrast between chaos and clarity is emotionally sticky

### 3. Visible Progression Across The Deck

Use:

- each slide should become more specific and more valuable

Reason:

- the viewer should feel they are moving deeper into the product, not sideways

### 4. Product-Proof Anchoring

Use:

- every claim should be anchored in a screenshot that proves it

Reason:

- product truth reduces skepticism
- screenshots make the story credible

### 5. Social Return Logic

Use:

- show that Felt is not just a one-time utility
- it is a place a group returns to

Reason:

- return intent is stronger when another person is part of the loop

### 6. Mastery Loop Framing

Use:

- show that Felt helps users get sharper

Reason:

- improvement is a cleaner retention engine than gambling-style excitement

### 7. Identity And Taste Signaling

Use:

- make the product feel like the clean, organized way thoughtful players run games

Reason:

- people keep and share tools that reflect well on them

## What Should Feel Addictive Here

The slideshow should feel addictive in the sense that:

- the viewer wants closure
- the viewer keeps getting rewarded for swiping
- the product looks increasingly useful
- the sequence feels intentional and premium

It should not feel addictive in the sense that:

- it exploits fear
- it implies money
- it uses gambling language
- it leans on sensory overload

## Slide-By-Slide Logic For The Next Version

### Slide 1: Hook The Pain

Psychological job:

- recognition
- tension
- curiosity

What the viewer should feel:

- "yes, that is the annoying part"

What to show:

- a clean hero state that suggests organization is possible
- not a busy table, not a broken flow

### Slide 2: Resolve It Fast

Psychological job:

- relief
- simplicity
- trust

What the viewer should feel:

- "oh, that is actually straightforward"

What to show:

- join with code
- host a table
- one-screen clarity

### Slide 3: Prove Operational Control

Psychological job:

- competence
- control
- credibility

What the viewer should feel:

- "this is a real system, not just a landing page"

What to show:

- live table state
- visible stacks, blinds, turn flow, or room structure

### Slide 4: Prove Improvement Value

Psychological job:

- aspiration
- mastery
- differentiation

What the viewer should feel:

- "this is not just for setup, it helps me sharpen up"

What to show:

- training mode
- feedback
- spot review

### Slide 5: Turn Usefulness Into Return Intent

Psychological job:

- invitation
- belonging
- return

What the viewer should feel:

- "my group could actually use this"

What to show:

- a clean return surface
- social continuity
- private game plus practice adjacency

## Design System Conclusions

### Color

Use:

- dark neutral base
- Felt green as the primary product color
- warm gold as a sparse attention accent
- truthful cream UI surfaces from the real product

Do not:

- flood the frame with gold
- use random bright colors with no role
- create casino-neon energy

### Composition

Use:

- one dominant screenshot per slide
- one support screenshot only if it adds proof
- a clear eye path from headline to proof

Do not:

- let proof assets overlap the reading path
- keep the same composition five times in a row
- use support crops that are too small to matter

### Copy

Use:

- short lines
- plain language
- structural language such as `home`, `code`, `room`, `crew`, `train`, `return`

Do not:

- oversell
- use vague superlatives
- use money, winnings, or casino-coded phrasing

## What To Keep From Current Work

- the general 5-slide narrative structure
- the real screenshot inventory
- the compliance lane
- the safe-zone and QA logic from the local HTML/export workflow

## What To Improve Next

- more distinctive slide-to-slide rhythm
- better screenshot states captured via Playwright
- better use of the strongest UI moments
- fewer decorative crops
- more assertive but still tasteful color contrast
- a stronger slide 1 to slide 2 curiosity handoff

## Critical Reasoning For The Next Agent

If a slide looks "premium" but does not increase swipe intent, it is not good enough.

If a slide creates curiosity but does not pay it off, it is not good enough.

If a slide has clean typography but weak proof, it is not good enough.

The winning version needs all three:

- desire to swipe
- immediate comprehension
- believable product proof
