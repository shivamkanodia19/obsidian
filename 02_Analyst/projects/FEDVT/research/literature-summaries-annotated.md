## FOUNDATIONAL PROFITABILITY STUDIES

### Paper 1: Determinants of Cattle Finishing Profitability
**Citation:** Langemeier, M. R., Schroeder, T. C., & Mintert, J. (1992). Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics, 24(2), 41–47.

**Key Finding:** 98% of profit variability explained by 6 variables (fed price, feeder price, corn, interest, FCR, ADG). Placement weight affects relative importance.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Justifies cost category structure. Cite in **Section 2** (Background) when explaining parameter selection.

---

### Paper 2: Elements of Cattle Feeding Profitability in Midwest Feedlots
**Citation:** Lawrence, J. D., Wang, Z., & Loy, D. (1999). Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics, 31(2), 349–357.

**Key Finding:** 1,600+ pens across 220 feedlots. Sex, placement weight, facility design, and season all significantly affect net returns beyond prices alone.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Supports breadth of 65-parameter input structure. Cite in **Section 3** (Materials & Methods) when explaining parameter selection beyond just prices.

---

### Paper 3: Identifying Economic Risk in Cattle Feeding
**Citation:** Mark, D. R., Schroeder, T. C., & Jones, R. (2000). Journal of Agribusiness, 18(3), 331–344.

**Key Finding:** Price risk dominates overall, but performance risk is proportionally larger for fall placements and lighter-weight cattle.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Primary justification for sensitivity analysis outputs. Cite in **Section 2** and **Section 4** (Results) when discussing sensitivity analysis visualization.

## KNOWLEDGE GAP & DECISION SUPPORT GAPS

### Paper 7: Recent Advances in Decision Support for Beef and Dairy Farming
**Citation:** Bang, A. L., Laugen, A. T., & Dreyer, H. C. (2023). International Transactions in Operational Research, 30(5), 2214–2244.

**Key Finding:** Systematic review of 110 studies (2016-2022). Gap between sophisticated models and user-friendly field tools persists. Most tools never move beyond proof-of-concept.

**Connection to FEDVT:** **SINGLE MOST IMPORTANT POSITIONING CITATION.** Comprehensive recent evidence of exact gap FEDVT addresses. Cite prominently in **Section 2** (Background and Literature Review) to establish the knowledge gap your paper fills.

---

### Paper 13: Structural Changes in Feedlot Performance Over Time
**Citation:** Herrington, M. A., & Tonsor, G. T. (2013). The Professional Animal Scientist, 29(4), 435–442.

**Key Finding:** Kansas Focus on Feedlots data over decades: ADG and FCE improved over time (genetic/nutrition advances), but margin volatility did NOT decrease correspondingly. Operating environment grew more complex, but tools remained static.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Knowledge gap citation. Feedlots became more technically sophisticated, yet tools available to operators remained static spreadsheets. FEDVT's interactive, scenario-capable design responds directly. Cite in **Section 2** (Background).

---

### Paper 15: Simulation Approaches Used for Management and Decision Making in the Beef Production Sector
**Citation:** Awasthi, T. R., Morshed, A., Williams, T., & Swain, D. L. (2024). Animals, 14(11), 1632.

**Key Finding:** Review of 105 simulation modeling studies in beef production. Most are deterministic/dynamic, but <50% include formal validation. Inconsistent reporting standards are main barrier to reproducibility and adoption.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Broad systems-level justification for simulation tools in beef production. Highlights methodological gaps FEDVT should address — particularly need for transparent documentation and validation. Cite in **Section 2** (Background).

## RISK & BASIS MANAGEMENT

### Paper 4: Management of Multiple Sources of Risk in Livestock Production
**Citation:** McKendree, M. G. S., Tonsor, G. T., & Schulz, L. L. (2021). Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics, 53(1), 75–93.

**Key Finding:** Feedlot operators manage output price risk and animal health risk jointly. Risk management strategies across different domains are complementary, not independent.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Supports Discussion section on stakeholder utility. Real operators manage multiple risk dimensions simultaneously — validates FEDVT's multi-scenario, multi-variable design. Cite in **Section 5** (Discussion).

---

### Paper 8: Feeder Cattle Basis Risk and Determinants
**Citation:** Bina, J. D., & Schroeder, T. C. (2022). Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics, 54(1), 1–21.

**Key Finding:** Weekly auction data from 32 feeder markets (1992-2021). Feeder cattle market volatility has statistically significant influence on basis risk. Basis peaked during 2014-2015 disruption, still elevated by 2018.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Supports Background section on why operators need producer-specific tools. Basis varies significantly by location and lot characteristics — tool starting from individual operation's own cost structure (like FEDVT) is more useful than relying on national futures alone. Cite in **Section 2** (Background).

---

### Paper 10: Impacts of Changes in Market Fundamentals and Price Momentum on Hedging Live Cattle
**Citation:** Coffey, B. K., Tonsor, G. T., & Schroeder, T. C. (2018). Journal of Agricultural and Resource Economics, 43(1), 18–33.

**Key Finding:** Basis prediction errors for live cattle across 5 major MLPR regions. Cost-of-gain volatility and delivery cost variation are LARGER contributors to hedging difficulty than market trends or price momentum. Results differ significantly across regions.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Reinforces case for FEDVT as individual-operation tool. Since hedging difficulty is driven primarily by local cost-of-gain volatility rather than national trends, operators need tool grounded in own cost structure. Cite in **Section 2** (Background) alongside Paper 9.

## RECENT DATA & FEED COST VOLATILITY

### Paper 6: Feed Prices, Substitution Patterns, and Technical Efficiency in Feedlot Cattle
**Citation:** Dennis, E. J., & Schroeder, T. C. (2023). Journal of Agricultural and Applied Economics, 55(4), 546–566.

**Key Finding:** Kansas Focus on Feedlots data (2015-2023). When corn prices rise, feedlots substitute toward alternative feedstuffs, changing FCR, ADG, and DOF. Net returns ranged from +$457 to -$489 per head — extreme margin volatility. Feed prices drive both cost structure AND biological performance.

**Connection to FEDVT:** **Most current data citation and strongest justification for FEDVT's existence.** The ±$489 per-head return range is powerful stat for Introduction. Validates feed cost sensitivity scenarios in Section 4 with real recent data. Cite in **Section 1** (Introduction) and **Section 4** (Results).

## PRICE & FORECASTING APPROACHES

### Paper 5: Multivariate Forecasting of a Commodity Portfolio: Application to Cattle Feeding Margins and Risk
**Citation:** Tonsor, G. T., & Schroeder, T. C. (2011). Applied Economics, 43(11), 1329–1339.

**Key Finding:** Feedlot managers operate in multivariate price environment (live cattle, feeder, corn simultaneously), yet most tools treat prices independently. Multivariate simulation accounting for price correlations significantly outperforms univariate approaches.

**Connection to FEDVT:** **Future development path.** Tool currently takes prices as user inputs without modeling joint behavior. Demonstrates what next generation should do — simulate correlated price scenarios across all three commodities. Cite in **Section 6** (Conclusion and Future Work).

---

### Paper 19: Using Probabilistic Machine Learning Methods to Improve Beef Cattle Price Modeling
**Citation:** Rahmani, E., Khatami, M., & Stephens, E. (2024). Sustainability, 16(5), 1789.

**Key Finding:** Compared ARIMA/SARIMA vs. random forest/AdaBoost for forecasting fed steer prices (2005-2023). Random forest outperformed univariate models. Added probabilistic layer for prediction intervals (showing uncertainty range), not just point estimates. Acknowledged limitations capturing extreme events.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Validates broader forecasting methodology and supports Future Work. Probabilistic forecasting intervals mirror FEDVT's future direction. Cite in **Section 2** (Background) for price forecasting context and **Section 6** (Future Work) for model uncertainty discussion.

## ANIMAL HEALTH & BRD ECONOMICS

### Paper 11: An Evaluation of the Economic Effects of BRD on Feedlot Cattle
**Citation:** Blakebrough-Hall, C., McMeniman, J. P., & González, L. A. (2020). Journal of Animal Science, 98(2), skaa045.

**Key Finding:** 898 crossbred steers in Australian feedlot. 18% morbidity, 2.1% BRD mortality. Cattle treated 3+ times: 39.6 kg lighter carcasses, AUD $385 less per head. Subclinical BRD (lung lesions, no treatment): AUD $67 per head loss. BRD costs often invisible to standard pen tracking.

**Connection to FEDVT:** **Primary BRD economics citation for disease outbreak use-case in Section 4.** Peer-reviewed benchmarks for realistic health cost inputs. Cite when demonstrating disease outbreak scenario to show outputs calibrated against real-world data.

---

### Paper 12: Market Impacts of Reducing the Prevalence of BRD in US Feedlots
**Citation:** Karle, A. S., Toth, J. D., & White, B. J. (2017). Frontiers in Veterinary Science, 4, 189.

**Key Finding:** NAHMS Beef Feedlot 2011 data: 21.2% of placed cattle affected by BRD at $23.60 per case treatment cost. Market-level partial equilibrium model: individual producers benefit from reduced BRD, but market-level price adjustments partially offset gains.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Industry-scale context for BRD's economic significance. $23.60 per-case and 21.2% morbidity are widely cited benchmarks for parameterizing health cost inputs in scenario demonstrations. Cite in **Section 2** (Background).

---

### Paper 17: Improving Feedlot Profitability Using Operational Data in Mortality Prediction Modeling
**Citation:** Feuz, R., Feuz, K., & Johnson, M. (2021). Journal of Agricultural and Resource Economics, 46(2), 242–255.

**Key Finding:** Machine learning models on 4,400+ cattle. Logistic regression best at 95.6% accuracy for mortality prediction. Economic analysis: using model increases net returns by $14.01/head for previously treated cattle; cost-sensitive learning pushes to $45.27/head with <1% loss probability.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Directly relevant to health/veterinary cost modeling and disease scenario use-case in Section 4. Demonstrates financial magnitude of data-driven health decisions. Provides concrete per-head dollar figures contextualizing FEDVT's health cost inputs. Cite in **Section 2** (Background) and **Section 4** (Results).

## ANALYTICS-BASED DECISION TOOLS

### Paper 16: Improvement of Feedlot Operations Through Statistical Learning and Business Analytics Tools
**Citation:** Flores, H., Meneses, C., Villalobos, J. R., & Sanchez, O. (2017). Computers and Electronics in Agriculture, 143, 273–285.

**Key Finding:** Statistical learning tool predicts individual animal growth and profitability using ultrasound backfat, marbling scores, feeding performance. Optimizes slaughter date per animal. Limited data insufficient — predictive models meaningfully improve feedlot efficiency.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Strong precedent showing analytics-based decision support tools for feedlot management have peer-reviewed validation. Positions FEDVT within emerging class of data-driven tools. Contrasts: Flores focuses on individual animal optimization; FEDVT operates at enterprise level. Cite in **Section 2** (Background).

---

### Paper 18: Optimizing Feedlot Placement Weights Using Simulation-Based Mathematical Programming
**Citation:** Stika, M. N. (2025). Kansas State University KREX repository.

**Key Finding:** Mathematical programming model using real-world Kansas feedlot data and Python. Optimal placement ~704 lbs with 166-day feeding, net returns ~$119/head. Incorporated all major variable costs (feeder, feed, yardage, health, interest) plus biological performance. Sensitivity analysis: feeder price and feed costs have largest impact on profitability.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Directly parallel to FEDVT's design objectives. Both translate feedlot cost structure into actionable placement/marketing decisions. Stika's finding that feeder price and feed cost dominate sensitivity INDEPENDENTLY validates FEDVT's emphasis on same parameters. Cite in **Section 2** (Background) as recent peer-reviewed counterpart.

## ECONOMIC ASSESSMENT STANDARDS & VALIDATION

### Paper 9: Economic Assessments from Experimental Research Trials of Feedlot Cattle Health and Performance: A Scoping Review
**Citation:** Dixon, A. L., Hanthorn, C. J., Pendell, D. L., Cernicchiaro, N., & Renter, D. G. (2022). Translational Animal Science, 6(3), txac077.

**Key Finding:** Scoping review of 113 feedlot trials from 91 peer-reviewed articles. Only 28% fully reported economic outcomes, methodology, price values, data sources together. 8 types of economic assessments identified (break-even, partial budgeting, enterprise analysis, profitability modeling). Inconsistency is barrier to comparability and stakeholder action.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Legitimizes FEDVT's design approach. Finding that break-even analysis and partial budgeting are most practically relevant economic frameworks validates FEDVT's deterministic break-even calculator as methodologically grounded. Supports Discussion section's argument that transparent, standardized tool improves on fragmented inconsistency. Cite in **Section 2** (Background) and **Section 5** (Discussion).

## MECHANISTIC MODELING & FUTURE INTEGRATION

### Paper 14: Price-Weight Relationships for Feeder Cattle
**Citation:** Dhuyvetter, K. C., & Schroeder, T. C. (2000). Canadian Journal of Agricultural Economics, 48(3), 299–310.

**Key Finding:** Canadian auction data. Price per pound declines as weight increases. Price-weight slide relationship shifts with corn prices and cattle market conditions. Quantified how slide steepens during high feed cost periods. Empirically estimated slide coefficients for budget/break-even calculations.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Directly relevant to feeder cattle purchase cost calculations (dominant cost driver in tool). Provides economic mechanism behind price-weight relationship operators must account for. Cite in **Section 3** (Materials and Methods) when explaining feeder cattle cost parameterization.

---

### Paper 20: Application of Models in Feedlot Systems: Maintenance Energy Requirements, Growth and Cost Curves, and Feeding Behavior
**Citation:** Harrison, M. A. (2022). ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Global (Order No. 29995958).

**Key Finding:** Applied Davis Growth Model to 120 Angus-cross steers with automated feeding systems. Modern cattle have higher maintenance energy/protein requirements than historical estimates. Developed profit and cost curves for optimal harvest timing. Sorting by predicted DOF rather than body weight improved carcass uniformity and reduced overfeeding costs.

**Connection to FEDVT:** Supports Background and Future Work sections. Harrison's use of cost curves and profit optimization for management output conceptually aligns with FEDVT's break-even and profitability projections. Call for individualized, data-driven management connects to FEDVT's future integration with IoT and precision livestock data (Section 5). Cite in **Section 2** and **Section 5**.
