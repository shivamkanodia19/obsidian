## 4 Question Types - Every Exam Has All 4

### TYPE 1: Polar Kinematics (Problem 1) - 25 pts

**Appears in:** EVERY exam (2005-2019), identical format all 15 years

**Exact question:** "Derive the expressions for the $\hat{r}$ and $\hat{\theta}$ components of the velocity and acceleration."

**What you derive:**
- $v_r = \dot{r}$
- $v_\theta = r\dot{\theta}$
- $a_r = \ddot{r} - r\dot{\theta}^2$
- $a_\theta = r\ddot{\theta} + 2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}$

**Difficulty:** Easy-Medium  
**Time:** 5-10 minutes  
**Strategy:** Memorize standard derivation, practice 2-3 times, **guaranteed 25 points**

**Examples:** 2007/1, 2008/1, 2009/1, 2015/1, 2019/1 (all identical)

---

### TYPE 2: Collisions & Momentum (Problem 2) - 12-25 pts

**Appears in:** EVERY exam (2005-2019)

**Collision Types:**

#### Inelastic (Objects Stick)
- Momentum conserved: $m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f$
- Energy lost
- **Examples:** 2007/2a (ball swings), 2009/2b (object sticks to block)

#### Elastic (Objects Bounce)
- Momentum AND energy conserved
- Two equations for two unknowns
- **Examples:** 2007/2b, 2009/2a (with springs)

#### Multi-Dimensional
- Conserve momentum in each direction
- Vector addition
- **Examples:** 2008/2b (sled collision at angle)

**Difficulty:** Easy-Medium to Medium  
**Time:** 6-10 minutes  
**Strategy:** Identify collision type → write momentum equation → add energy equation if elastic

**All Collision Problems:**
- 2005/2 (disks), 2006/2 (blocks), 2007/2, 2008/2, 2009/2, 2010/2, 2011/2, 2012/3, 2013/2, 2014/2, 2015/2, 2016/2, 2017/2, 2018/2, 2019/2

---

### TYPE 3: Angular Momentum (Problem 3) - 8-25 pts

**Appears in:** EVERY exam (2005-2019)

**Core Concept:** $L = I\omega = \text{constant}$ (when no external torques)

**Main Scenarios:**

#### Moment of Inertia Changes (Most Common)
- Person pulls arms in → I decreases → ω increases
- Mass moves toward/away from axis
- Apply: $I_i \omega_i = I_f \omega_f$
- **Examples:** 2007/3b (arms pull in), 2008/3a (mass moves toward axis)

#### Angular Momentum About a Point
- Flying plane or object in space
- Formula: $L = \vec{r} \times m\vec{v}$
- **Examples:** 2008/4a (electron orbit), 2009/4a (plane)

#### Composite Systems
- Rod + masses rotating together
- Total I = rod's I + masses' I
- **Examples:** 2007/3c, 2008/3c, 2009/4b

**Difficulty:** Easy-Medium to Medium-Hard  
**Time:** 5-12 minutes  
**Strategy:** Identify if I changes → set up $I_i \omega_i = I_f \omega_f$ → solve for unknown

**Key Moment of Inertia Formulas:**
- Point mass at distance r: $I = mr^2$
- Rod about center: $I = \frac{1}{12}ML^2$
- Rod about end: $I = \frac{1}{3}ML^2$
- Solid cylinder: $I = \frac{1}{2}MR^2$

---

### TYPE 4: Rotational Dynamics & Torque (Problem 4) - 6-25 pts

**Appears in:** EVERY exam (2005-2019)

**Core Equations:**
- Torque: $\tau = rF\sin\theta = r_\perp F$
- Newton's 2nd (rotation): $\tau = I\alpha$
- Tangential acceleration: $a_t = r\alpha$
- Centripetal acceleration: $a_c = \omega^2 r$

**Main Scenarios:**

#### Rod Rotating About Hinge
- Gravity acts at center (L/2)
- Different forces at different angles
- **Examples:** 2007/4 (4 sub-parts: horizontal/vertical, torque/acceleration)

#### Circular Motion / Conveyor Belts
- Both tangential and centripetal forces
- Friction constraints
- **Examples:** 2009/3a-c (box on circular conveyor belt)

#### Gravity & Rotation
- Pulley systems, blocks hanging
- Combines rotational + linear motion
- **Examples:** 2005/4, 2006/4

**Difficulty:** Easy to Hard  
**Time:** 4-10 minutes  
**Strategy:** Identify forces → calculate perpendicular distance → apply $\tau = I\alpha$

**Key Insights:**
- Torque = 0 when force passes through axis
- Torque = 0 when force parallel to radius
- Centripetal vs. tangential acceleration matters

## Key Formulas (Quick Reference)

### Polar Coordinates (MEMORIZE)
- $v_r = \dot{r}$, $v_\theta = r\dot{\theta}$
- $a_r = \ddot{r} - r\dot{\theta}^2$, $a_\theta = r\ddot{\theta} + 2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}$

### Momentum & Collisions
- $p = mv$ (momentum)
- Inelastic: $m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v_f$
- Elastic: momentum + energy both conserved

### Angular Motion
- $L = I\omega$ (angular momentum)
- $\tau = I\alpha$ (torque = moment × acceleration)
- $I_{rod,center} = \frac{1}{12}ML^2$, $I_{rod,end} = \frac{1}{3}ML^2$

### Circular Motion
- $a_c = \frac{v^2}{r} = \omega^2 r$ (centripetal)
- $v = r\omega$ (tangential velocity)
- $F_c = m\omega^2 r$ (centripetal force)

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Polar coordinates:** Forgetting the $-r\dot{\theta}^2$ term in $a_r$
2. **Collisions:** Mixing up elastic (energy conserved) vs. inelastic (energy lost)
3. **Angular momentum:** Forgetting that I can change over time
4. **Torque:** Using full force instead of perpendicular component
5. **Signs:** Wrong sign in centripetal acceleration direction
